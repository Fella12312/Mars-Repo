The Mission

WHY MARS?

Mars is the most Earth-like planet in our solar system. Billions of years ago, it had liquid water, a thicker atmosphere, and conditions that may have supported microbial life. Today, its thin, cold atmosphere and barren deserts hold clues to the history of planetary evolution — and perhaps to life itself.

NASA's Mars rover program has been one of the most ambitious scientific endeavors in human history. Each rover is a rolling laboratory, equipped with cameras, spectrometers, drills, and weather stations — conducting experiments that would require hundreds of scientists working simultaneously on Earth.

The data gathered by these rovers has reshaped our understanding of Mars: we've confirmed ancient lake beds, detected organic molecules, found seasonal methane spikes, and captured the first powered flight on another planet. Every drive is a discovery.

The Explorers

EVERY ROVER,
EVERY MISSION

Five rovers have successfully landed on Mars. Each generation more capable than the last — from the pioneering Sojourner to the car-sized Perseverance still roaming today.

1997
Sojourner
Mission Complete

The first rover on Mars, Sojourner operated for 83 Martian days — far beyond its planned 7-day mission. Weighing just 10.6 kg, it proved that robotic rovers could successfully traverse alien terrain. It analysed rocks with an alpha proton X-ray spectrometer, revolutionising our understanding of Martian geology.

2004
Spirit
Mission Complete

Spirit landed in Gusev Crater, originally believed to be a former lake bed. While Gusev yielded few aqueous minerals at first, Spirit's climb up the Columbia Hills uncovered compelling evidence of ancient water activity. Spirit operated for over 2,200 Martian days before becoming stuck in soft soil in 2009.

2004
Opportunity
Mission Complete

Opportunity landed in Eagle Crater and quickly found hematite "blueberries" — mineral spheres formed in liquid water. It then drove for 45 km across Meridiani Planum over 15 years, outlasting its 90-day mission by a factor of 60. A global dust storm in 2018 finally ended its extraordinary run.

2012
Curiosity
Active

The size of a car, Curiosity redefined what a Mars rover could do. Landing via a sky crane in Gale Crater, it confirmed that Mars once had a habitable environment. It detected organic molecules, measured radiation levels for future astronauts, and is still exploring Mount Sharp more than a decade after landing.

2021
Perseverance
Active

NASA's most advanced rover, Perseverance landed in Jezero Crater — an ancient river delta. It collected rock samples for future return to Earth, deployed the Ingenuity helicopter (the first powered aircraft on another planet), and produced breathable oxygen from the Martian atmosphere using the MOXIE experiment.
